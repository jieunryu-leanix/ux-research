## 1. Executive Summary

The Enterprise Architecture (EA) tools market is a small but structurally important segment inside enterprise software, sized between USD 1.1 billion and USD 1.3 billion in 2024 and projected to grow at a compound annual rate of roughly four to six percent through 2030. Despite the modest headline size, the segment has become one of the most strategically active corners of enterprise IT in the last two years, driven by three converging forces: the SAP acquisition of LeanIX in November 2023 for an estimated USD 1.2 to 1.4 billion, a wave of vendor consolidation that combined Bizzdesign, MEGA International, and Alfabet under a single roof in 2025, and a rapid pivot by every major vendor toward AI-native product architectures anchored on Model Context Protocol (MCP) servers, retrieval-augmented generation, and embedded copilots. Within this landscape, SAP LeanIX and Ardoq remain the two most-cited direct rivals, both positioned in the Leaders quadrant of Gartner's 2025 Magic Quadrant for EA Tools, with LeanIX claiming the furthest and highest position on both Completeness of Vision and Ability to Execute and Ardoq ranked first for the Enterprise Transformation and Portfolio Management use case. The two products are near-parity on public rating platforms (G2 4.5/5 for both; Gartner Peer Insights 4.7 for LeanIX historically and 4.8 currently for Ardoq) but diverge sharply on philosophy, metamodel flexibility, decision management maturity, and roadmapping paradigm. LeanIX pairs an opinionated fact-sheet–driven metamodel with fast time-to-value and deep SAP ecosystem integration; Ardoq offers a graph-native, branch-based scenario engine, a first-class Architecture Records solution shipping since roughly 2021, and a distinctive crowdsourcing loop built from Broadcasts, Discover, and Surveys. On Architecture Decision Management, both vendors now ship native modules — Ardoq's is more mature and integrated with Jira initiatives; LeanIX's was launched in 2025 and is being progressively enhanced with AI guidance and diagram embedding. On Target Architecture Planning and Roadmapping, Ardoq's Scenarios (compared by ThoughtWorks to Git-style branches) are the analyst-endorsed differentiator, while LeanIX embeds Gartner's TIME and 6R rationalization frameworks as first-class fields and offers unlimited users across all tiers to structurally enable roadmap sharing. On AI Governance and AI-assisted features, LeanIX has taken a first-mover position with the AI Agent Hub and MCP Server going generally available in 2025 as included subscription features, an AI Governance & Adoption metamodel extension that captures EU AI Act risk tiers, and deep integration with SAP Joule; Ardoq counters with an Omnipresent AI Assistant, an AI Gateway MCP server, AI-powered relationship inference via RAG, and Foundation Insights agents. On UX and adoption, LeanIX is repeatedly praised as easier to use, easier to set up, and easier to administer, especially for non-EA business users, while Ardoq is repeatedly praised for flexibility, cross-domain coverage, and best-in-class customer support (Gartner Peer Insights 4.9/5), but repeatedly criticized for a steep learning curve and busy UI. Emerging threats are less about a single AI-native disruptor and more about a three-vector encroachment: Ardoq itself (with graph plus AI plus a GraphLake acquisition creating a neuro-symbolic stack), AI-native startups such as Catio and Boldo that are still small but philosophically differentiated, and developer portals (Backstage, Cortex, OpsLevel, Roadie, Atlassian Compass, Port.io) that increasingly own the software catalog wedge that once anchored EA. For SAP LeanIX, the strategic implications are clear: defend and extend the AI Governance moat, address the three most cited product weaknesses (metamodel rigidity, reporting polish, and Business Architecture depth), and use the SAP-scale go-to-market to neutralize the "SAP will make LeanIX SAP-only" perception that competitors are actively exploiting.

## 2. Market Landscape and Analyst Positions

### 2.1 Market size and growth

The EA software market shows a consistent picture across multiple independent research houses. Grand View Research sizes the market at USD 1.14 billion in 2024, rising to USD 1.60 billion by 2030 at a 6.0 percent CAGR. Polaris Market Research pegs 2024 at USD 1.23 billion and forecasts USD 2.22 billion by 2034 at 6.1 percent CAGR. Market Research Future reports USD 1.27 billion in 2024 with a 5.0 percent CAGR to 2035, while Business Research Insights sees the market crossing USD 1.4 billion by 2028 with "a CAGR of over six percent." Mordor Intelligence provides the most conservative outlook at 3.82 percent CAGR through 2030. Taken together, the workshop-ready consensus is that the market is roughly USD 1.1 to 1.3 billion in 2024, expanding to around USD 1.4 to 1.6 billion by 2028, with 4 to 6 percent annual growth. This is a small market by enterprise-software standards, which explains both the pricing power the leading vendors enjoy and the intensity of the consolidation dynamic.

### 2.2 Gartner Magic Quadrant for EA Tools (2024 and 2025)

Gartner's 2024 Magic Quadrant placed both Ardoq and SAP LeanIX in the Leaders quadrant. Analyst summary reporting positions LeanIX furthest on the Completeness of Vision axis, with recurring strengths cited as application portfolio management, SAP integration, ease of adoption, and a "very intuitive interface and fast time to value." Recurring cautions include the observation that LeanIX is "lighter in Business Architecture and Data Architecture than some peers" and that "organizations may outgrow LeanIX if they later need more flexible modeling or broader EA depth." Ardoq scored highly in Gartner's companion Critical Capabilities report for Repository and Data Management, Intelligent Automation, and Presentation. Analysts praise Ardoq's flexible metamodel and graph foundation, scenario/future-state modeling, and stakeholder engagement via the Discover broadcast features, but caution that "designing the metamodel can be demanding" and that implementation timelines expand when scope is broad.

The 2025 Gartner Magic Quadrant tells a story of dramatic consolidation. Only ten vendors were assessed, with "three new entrants and five departures due to mergers, acquisitions, and consolidations." Five vendors were named Leaders: Avolution (ABACUS), Ardoq, Bizzdesign, SAP LeanIX, and BOC Group (ADOIT). Avolution was recognized as a Leader for the ninth consecutive year and received the highest score for EA Governance in the accompanying Critical Capabilities report. Ardoq's Leader position for a fifth consecutive year was accompanied by a first-place ranking for the Enterprise Transformation and Portfolio Management use case and a second-place ranking for Advanced Roadmapping; Ardoq's own press positions the company as "the only Leader combining EA and BPM." Bizzdesign, in its first year post-merger with MEGA International and Alfabet, was called a Leader for the eighteenth year and placed "among the three highest-ranked vendors across all use cases." SAP LeanIX claimed the strongest headline in its press release, stating it is "positioned furthest and highest" on both axes and emphasizing application and IT portfolio management, sustainability tracking, and data-driven technology investment as differentiators. BOC Group's ADOIT was noted for "significant progress on the Ability to Execute scale in 2025" and, distinctively, differentiated on "integrated AI functionalities embedded into EA use cases at no extra cost."

### 2.3 Forrester Wave for EAMS (Q4 2024)

The Q4 2024 Forrester Wave for Enterprise Architecture Management Suites evaluated twelve vendors and named four Leaders: Orbus Software, MEGA International, Bizzdesign, and Software AG. Notably, neither SAP LeanIX nor Ardoq was named a Leader in that Wave. Orbus Software scored highest in Current Offering at 3.92 and jointly highest in Strategy at 3.80, and was one of only two vendors with above-average customer feedback. Bizzdesign tied for the second-highest score in both Current Offering and Strategy. The Forrester ranking contrasts sharply with Gartner's, which reflects the different assessment methodologies but is a meaningful data point for buyers doing analyst diligence. The prior Q1 2023 Wave had also evaluated twelve vendors — with Orbus a Leader and Ardoq's commentary noting the company "needs to improve out-of-the-box modeling and security architecture capabilities to improve its market position" — indicating steady if uneven analyst progression for both LeanIX and Ardoq relative to the more established modeling vendors.

### 2.4 SAP acquisition of LeanIX

SAP's acquisition of LeanIX was announced on 7 September 2023 and closed in November 2023. Deal terms were officially undisclosed. TechCrunch reported the price "in the same neighborhood as SAP's Signavio purchase (1.2 billion dollars) but a bit higher." Sifted called it "one of Germany's largest ever tech acquisitions" with an estimated USD 1.2 billion value. E3 Magazine cited "around one billion EUR." The best-supported figure is approximately EUR 1 billion or USD 1.2 to 1.4 billion. LeanIX pre-acquisition metrics indicate approximately EUR 100 million in revenue for 2023 (interpreted as roughly EUR 100 million ARR given the pure SaaS model), approximately 1,000 customers per TechCrunch (frequently cited as 1,200 in industry commentary), approximately 600 employees, and around 50 percent of revenue from the United States. The implied valuation multiple of roughly 12 times revenue was described as "in line with high-growth SaaS deals in EAM/process management." SAP had itself been a LeanIX customer since 2017. Germany's Bundeskartellamt cleared the deal in first-phase review, finding the products "complementary" and identifying "no significant impediment to competition." SAP is now positioning LeanIX as a core component of RISE with SAP and S/4HANA transformation programs, part of a broader "transformation suite" that combines LeanIX (EA and applications) with Signavio (process) and WalkMe (guidance). Commercially, SAP has retained LeanIX's core pricing metrics of fact sheets plus named users but "integrated them into SAP's standard list price framework, meaning legacy LeanIX discount benchmarks no longer apply." Customer reception has been mixed: analysts described the fit as "strategically positive," while Reddit and E3 Magazine commentary raised churn concerns tied to neutrality perceptions and pricing changes.

### 2.5 The wider competitive set

Beyond LeanIX and Ardoq, the recurring competitive set for 2024 to 2026 includes Bizzdesign Horizzon (now consolidated with MEGA HOPEX and Software AG Alfabet under the Bizzdesign brand as of 2025), Orbus Software's OrbusInfinity and iServer365, Planview Enterprise One, Sparx Systems' Enterprise Architect with Prolaborate, Avolution's ABACUS (ranked number one for EA Governance in Gartner's 2025 Critical Capabilities), BOC Group's ADOIT (which markets AI-embedded EA with no additional cost), QualiWare, erwin Evolve from Quest, ServiceNow's rapidly evolving EA module, ValueBlue Blue Dolphin, and niche players such as Atoll SAMU and Cloudsoft AMP. The Forrester Q4 2024 assessment noted that M&A activity "started accelerating in 2023, continued through 2024, and reached a new peak entering 2025, reshaping the EAMS market." Notable transactions include SAP's LeanIX acquisition, North Highland's acquisition of UMT360, Bizzdesign's absorption of Edifit, Silver Lake's take-private of Software AG followed by the Alfabet divestiture, Sparx Systems' acquisition of Prolaborate in January 2024, Ardoq's acquisition of ShiftX in September 2024, Orbus Software's acquisition of Capsifi in December 2024, SAP's WalkMe acquisition, and the 2025 consolidation of Bizzdesign, MEGA International, and Alfabet under the Bizzdesign brand.

## 3. Architecture Decision Management: LeanIX vs. Ardoq vs. the Field

### 3.1 Why Architecture Decision Management matters now

Architecture Decision Records emerged from Michael Nygard's 2011 "Documenting Architecture Decisions" essay, which proposed a five-section template (Title, Status, Context, Decision, Consequences) and framed decisions as append-only artifacts written for a future developer. The Markdown Any Decision Records (MADR) extension, currently on version 4.x, adds structured fields such as Deciders, Date, Decision Drivers, Considered Options, and Decision Outcome, and is "best when you need formal evaluation of alternatives, clear traceability, and comparability across decisions" — particularly for "larger teams, regulated environments, or complex architecture choices." ThoughtWorks placed lightweight ADRs in the Adopt ring on its Technology Radar and later extended the pattern to lightweight RFCs, advising teams to "store [them] in source control, not a wiki" so that decisions stay in sync with code. The reason ADRs matter for EA vendors is precisely that the developer community has proven the pattern's value and expects the same rigor at the enterprise level; if EA tools cannot host decisions in a way that is at least as good as Markdown in Git, they will lose the argument entirely to standalone tools such as Structurizr, Log4brains, and adr-tools.

### 3.2 Ardoq — Architecture Records / Decision Records solution

Ardoq ships a dedicated Architecture Records / Decision Records solution as part of its Solutions catalog. Each decision is modeled as a first-class component in Ardoq's knowledge graph, with a customizable component type such as "Decision Record" or "Architecture Record" in its own workspace. Standard fields include the title, description with context and consequences, approval status (Proposed, Under Review, Approved, Rejected, Superseded), created and decision and last-updated dates, an owner referenced to a Person component, and an optional document URL for a "twin" ADR page in Confluence or SharePoint. Authoring workflows include direct creation by architects, survey-driven capture from non-EA contributors, and generation as part of scenario evaluation such as an Azure-versus-AWS options analysis. Governance and consumer views include a dedicated Discover Viewpoint called "Decision Records" that publishes the decision log to stakeholders, conditional formatting based on approval status, and appearance in pre-go-live governance dashboards alongside risk and health metrics. The Confluence integration is deliberately shallow: rather than a deep bidirectional sync, each ADR record carries a Document URL to a Confluence twin page — Ardoq acts as the structured relational repository while Confluence holds the narrative. The Jira integration is deeper: ADRs are linked to initiatives, and initiatives are integrated with Jira, surfacing execution status and blockers alongside the decision. Governance dashboards can therefore show ADR-to-work-item-to-delivery-status traceability, which is a distinctive value proposition. The Ardoq Solutions Change Log indicates the solution has been shipping since approximately 2021, giving Ardoq roughly three to four years of maturity ahead of LeanIX on the feature.

### 3.3 SAP LeanIX — Architecture Decisions

SAP LeanIX's Architecture Decisions feature was announced in 2025 as part of the Architecture Suite. The messaging emphasizes storing "architecture decision records in one place instead of scattering them across email, spreadsheets, Confluence, or SharePoint," speeding up decision-making, and supporting asynchronous collaboration between architects and Architecture Review Boards. Decisions integrate with Fact Sheets, allowing decisions to be tied to Applications, IT Components, Projects, Capabilities, and other artifacts. The 2025 roadmap listed AI-based architecture guidance as a planned enhancement, and later 2025 announcements extended the feature with diagram embedding to improve scenario planning and option comparison. Field structure infers to include title, decision type (technology choice, integration pattern, deprecation, standardization), status (Proposed / Accepted / Rejected / Superseded / Draft), date, author, stakeholders, problem statement and context, options and alternatives considered, rationale, and links to Fact Sheets. Given the 2025 launch, LeanIX has less than a year of production maturity on this feature versus Ardoq's approximately four years, but LeanIX's advantage is that decisions become first-class citizens inside the SAP transformation stack, with the AI guidance overlay directly on the roadmap. A verbatim customer quote from a LinkedIn recap of the MCP Server release captures the direction of travel: "You can now use natural language to chat with compliant AI agents to retrieve Fact Sheet data, find reports, manage surveys, and even create architecture decisions."

### 3.4 Other EA tools

MEGA HOPEX has no evidence of a dedicated ADR module in public documentation; decisions are typically captured through custom metamodel objects, attached documents, and workflow-based approvals inside the HOPEX governance framework. Bizzdesign Horizzon shows no branded ADR feature in public sources; decisions are captured via configurable object types and linked to ArchiMate elements, with no productized approval workflow or Confluence/Git integration. Orbus iServer has no dedicated ADR module either — practitioners typically define a metamodel element called "Architecture Decision Record," capture attributes such as title, status, date, owner, decision summary, and consequences, and link decisions to Applications, Interfaces, Technologies, and Business Capabilities, while storing narrative ADRs externally in Git or Markdown. These gaps are meaningful: on this specific feature, only Ardoq and LeanIX have branded, first-class ADR modules among the major EA vendors.

### 3.5 Standalone and open-source ADR tools

Structurizr, Simon Brown's models-as-code C4 platform, has explicit ADR support via a DSL keyword that attaches a folder of ADRs to a workspace, software system, or container. Structurizr Lite "can render ADRs in the browser, including a searchable view and navigation between ADRs, and can visualize superseded decisions as a graph." Supported input formats include adr-tools, MADR, Log4brains, and custom importers. Log4brains is an open-source docs-as-code ADR tool with a CLI, Markdown-in-Git storage, static Next.js site publishing, date-based filenames (YYYYMMDD-adr-name.md to reduce merge conflicts), a Draft status for pre-decision collaboration, and Tags for classification; when wired into CI, each merge to the ADR directory regenerates the site so the published ADR library is always up to date. The adr-tools Bash CLI by npryce is the original toolkit and auto-maintains monotonic numbering and superseding links. adr-manager provides a web UI, adr-viewer generates a website from a set of ADRs, and ADMentor is a Sparx Enterprise Architect add-in providing a UML profile and MDG Technology extension that supports Y-Statements, Question-Option-Criteria diagrams, and Problem Space and Solution Space modeling, aligned with ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010. The Confluence-plus-Jira pattern remains the default for enterprises without a dedicated EA tool, using an Architecture Decision Record blueprint labeled "adr," Application Links with OAuth for bidirectional integration, Jira Issues macros for live dashboards, and Jira automation to block certain epic types unless an "Accepted" ADR is linked.

### 3.6 The strategic gap for LeanIX

Both Ardoq and LeanIX have branded ADR modules, but neither yet ships a Git-native ADR sync that provides round-trip Markdown-to-workspace synchronization — the pattern developer teams actually want and where Structurizr's DSL importer already wins. This is the frontier where AI-assisted decision drafting will meet code-native governance, and it is the differentiation battleground for 2026. Verbatim customer or analyst quotes about decision management specifically are sparse in the public web crawl; workshop attribution for external-facing claims about competitor sentiment on ADR features would benefit from primary customer interviews.

## 4. Target Architecture Planning: A Feature-by-Feature Comparison

### 4.1 The Ardoq scenario model

Ardoq organizes target-state work around a small set of first-class primitives layered on its graph. The mainline architecture is the trusted as-is graph, kept as accurate as possible. Scenarios are "an isolated branch of your data where you model potential changes (what-if analysis) without touching live data," allowing an entire model or a subset such as a single domain to be branched, modified, and compared against mainline via visual diff. Future State components represent "options, transition states, or target states along the path to the desired outcome" and can be target-state solutions, transitional architectures, or alternative options for evaluation. Design Objects are used for conceptual or reference elements not yet in the operational metamodel. ThoughtWorks Radar frames Scenarios in a way that lands with engineering audiences, "comparing its branching/merging approach to Git-style branches for future-state modeling." Roadmapping in Ardoq is scenario-driven rather than a dedicated Gantt module: architects create a scenario from current state, define project phases connected to impacted systems, add start and end dates and milestones, visualize the timeline of system impacts per phase, and roll it up into a Presentation for stakeholders. Multiple concurrent roadmaps are supported and stay in sync with mainline, reducing the manual diagram-maintenance drift that reviewers criticize other tools for. Future states can be assessed with quality models to identify risk, technical debt, and outcome validation, and capability heatmaps are auto-generated projections of the graph rather than standalone artifacts, so a scenario workspace and the as-is workspace can be diffed directly.

### 4.2 The LeanIX portfolio-and-initiatives model

LeanIX's target-architecture capability has been renamed and repackaged several times: the base Application Portfolio Management module (fact sheets, TIME/6R support, dashboards, surveys, integrations), Architecture and Road Map Planning for to-be state and initiatives, the Business Transformation Module (BTM) as an add-on for scenarios and impact modeling, and the newer branded experience Target Architecture Planning and Diagrams under SAP LeanIX for defining to-be architecture, identifying gaps, and communicating targets with reports and KPIs. Time-based visualization is supported through Roadmap reports with Gantt-style application and technology lifecycle views by phase (plan, phase-in, active, phase-out, end-of-life), an Initiative Fact Sheet that carries future states, milestones, conflicts, and linked applications and capabilities, what-if analysis before commitment, and milestone tracking across initiatives. Gartner's TIME model (Tolerate, Invest, Migrate, Eliminate) and the 6R cloud migration classifications (rehost, replatform, rearchitect, repurchase, retain, retire) are embedded as first-class fields on the Application Fact Sheet, giving LeanIX a distinctive Gartner-alignment story out of the box. Capability heatmaps colored by functional fit, technical fit, cost, or business criticality are consistently cited as executive-communication artifacts. Free Draw was added to bridge LeanIX's historically catalog-first model, allowing ArchiMate, C4, and UML palettes with fact-sheet containers filtered by repository attributes, though with two important limitations: ordinary shapes that are not fact sheets "are not part of the LeanIX data model; they won't appear in reports or other views," and relations drawn visually "that do not exist in the metamodel remain purely visual, not data."

### 4.3 Reviewer verdict on roadmapping strengths and weaknesses

Reviewer language on the strengths of each vendor is telling. A LeanIX G2 reviewer writes: "I use SAP LeanIX for road map planning, enabling transparency, reducing IT spend, managing risks, and linking business capabilities to solutions," while another highlights "the ability to offer a structured framework that helps define a target architecture and develop a transformation plan." On weaknesses, one G2 reviewer captures a widely echoed critique: "More geared towards a one-time transformation project… ongoing annual cycles can be more of a challenge because the product assigns responsibilities to individuals rather than organizational positions." Another observes that "some reports are still not visually appealing enough to present to C-level stakeholders" and that "the diagram editor is very basic but improving rapidly. E.g. Copy and Paste has only just been introduced into beta." Ardoq reviewers celebrate the flexibility — "Ardoq itself is extremely flexible compared to most other EA tools (being really a graph database with a fancy front-end!), meaning we can quickly create relationships between completely disparate reference datasets" — but consistently flag the same weaknesses: "UI/UX can feel overwhelming, tied to the flexibility of the underlying graph model," and filters "do not always help," with "data being filtered away without them noticing, creating confusion when analyzing views."

### 4.4 Crowdsourcing target-state input

A substantial Ardoq strength on target architecture is the crowdsourcing loop LeanIX does not match natively. Broadcasts are described as Ardoq's "highly configurable automated messaging platform" for distributing survey invitations and reminders at scale, targeting audiences via Gremlin queries, and driving workflows including approvals. Surveys are positioned as the module for "crowdsourcing information from the broadest possible contributor base." Ardoq Discover is the lightweight consumer front-end — non-EA users receive an email link, land on a Component Overview page, and complete surveys inline. A My Tasks widget in Discover consolidates all survey tasks per user, and Survey Response Approvals allow dynamic approver assignment (users, groups, or relationships) to validate survey data before it is committed. LeanIX's Surveys write responses directly into Fact Sheets and can be sent to internal and external recipients regardless of whether they use LeanIX, with drag-and-drop questionnaire design, conditional questions, JavaScript calculated fields, Excel export, and automations that can auto-assign tags and generate quality-assurance tasks. Both approaches are effective; Ardoq's is more flexible and portal-based, and LeanIX's is more structured and enforced.

### 4.5 The wider roadmapping market

Planview Enterprise One connects strategy, business capabilities, investments, and technology, with capability modeling linked to strategic objectives and capability roadmaps that enhance or create capabilities needed for strategic outcomes; strategic roadmaps define timeframes, milestones, and release dates across portfolios. Planview is stronger when PMO and PPM plus financial planning is the anchor, weaker on EA metamodel and graph analysis. Bizzdesign Horizzon is ArchiMate-native and plateau-based, modeling current, intermediate, and target states with capability increments and plateaus (an "architecture state valid over a period of time with a start and end date") as the core roadmap primitive; initiatives link to plateaus and capabilities and roadmaps auto-update. ServiceNow Strategic Portfolio Management adds a Strategic Planning workspace with goals and KPIs and scenarios, an Alignment Planner for enterprise or portfolio roadmaps mixing projects, demands, and epics, plus an APM module with application inventory, health and value scoring, capability alignment, cloud assessment, and application roadmaps — all integrated through CSDM and CMDB. MEGA HOPEX supports transformation stages as phased roadmaps aligned with capability, vision, objectives, strategy, and tactics per stage, with dependency and prioritization analysis and current-versus-future-state modeling.

### 4.6 The head-to-head roadmapping comparison

| Dimension | LeanIX | Ardoq |
|---|---|---|
| Core philosophy | Portfolio visibility and structured transformation | Graph-based, data-driven, dependency-centric |
| Target architecture modeling | APM + Architecture and Road Map Planning + BTM; templates for ERP and app modernization | Future State components + Scenarios; user-defined model |
| Roadmapping | Initiative-linked roadmaps, milestones, conflict visibility, what-if analysis | Scenario-driven roadmaps with side-by-side option comparison |
| Metamodel | Opinionated, controlled extensibility | Fully flexible graph |
| Free-form modeling | Free Draw layered on fact sheets; non-fact-sheet shapes are visual-only | Canvas is graph; every relation drawn is queryable data |
| Crowdsourcing | Surveys (basic) | Broadcasts + Discover + Surveys + Approvals |
| Integrations focus | Application portfolio and SAP ecosystem; strong Jira sync | Cloud, CMDB, DevOps; live sync with Jira and ServiceNow |
| Ease of use | Rated easier to set up; structured onboarding | Complex setup; powerful once configured |
| Roadmap sharing | Unlimited viewer users, dashboards | Presentations + Discover consumer app |

The reviewer verdict on LeanIX's roadmap weakness ("more geared towards a one-time transformation project") and Ardoq's target-state weakness ("UI/UX can feel overwhelming… filters do not always help") point to the same underlying tension: target architecture planning requires both structure for adoption and flexibility for real-world evolution, and neither vendor has fully solved it. That is precisely the strategic gap the workshop should exploit.

## 5. AI Governance and AI Features: A Deep Dive

### 5.1 SAP LeanIX AI capabilities

SAP LeanIX has taken an aggressive first-mover position on AI-native EA infrastructure. The Enterprise Architecture Assistant provides natural-language Q&A grounded in customer fact sheets, architecture decisions, Confluence, PDFs, and selected external sources, with use cases including auto-completing fact sheets, researching alternative technologies, building transformation roadmaps, and producing AI-guided consolidation and retirement recommendations. As of 2025 the assistant itself is in beta while the underlying MCP and agent infrastructure is generally available; full assistant general availability is stabilizing into 2026. The underlying LLM was originally a dedicated GPT-3.5-Turbo model hosted on Microsoft Azure tailored for EA use cases; it has since been expanded to SAP Business AI and the GenAI Hub, which hosts multiple LLMs including GPT-4-Turbo, Google Gemini, Anthropic Claude, Cohere, Perplexity models, Alibaba Qwen in China, and the SAP Foundation Model.

Base AI capabilities included in the standard EA subscription are the Inventory AI Prompt for natural-language querying, an "Add filter with AI" free-text query interpreter, AI-assisted text that auto-generates and refines fact-sheet descriptions with SAP reporting up to approximately 80 percent reduction in time to fill application descriptions, AI-generated context around tasks and surveys and to-dos, and AI-supported translation for multilingual labels and help texts. The one explicitly premium and gated AI feature is the Inventory Builder, which analyzes diagrams, images, and (extending in 2025) documents and spreadsheets to auto-extract architectural elements and generate fact sheets and relationships via an inbox review workflow; activation requires Optional Features enablement plus acceptance of AI-specific terms.

The AI Agent Hub, generally available in 2025 and confirmed by SAP LeanIX leadership on LinkedIn with the verbatim statement "We've launched the SAP LeanIX MCP Server and the AI Agent Hub… both now Generally Available (GA) as of today and included in the subscription for all customers!," is the flagship AI Governance capability. The SAP community recap explains: "Last week, we announced the general availability of the AI agent hub in SAP LeanIX. All existing customers can start discovering their agents from Microsoft, Google, and SAP immediately." Capabilities include a central inventory for SAP-delivered agents, custom Joule agents, and third-party agents (Microsoft Copilot, Google, custom-built); discovery via out-of-box integrations for AI agents and MCP servers; a metamodel that captures agent skills, capabilities, authentication modes, risk, approval state, and business value; an AI Agent Radar that visualizes approval status (exploratory, preview, fully accepted) to prevent uncontrolled AI sprawl; and Architecture Decision Records that attach governance rationale, risk, and constraints per agent. Pricing is a notable commercial detail: the AI Agent Hub is available for all workspaces without additional product license, with pricing that counts five AI agents as one application against subscription quotas.

The AI Governance and Adoption metamodel extension, available under Optional Features and Early Access, adds a dedicated AI Governance section to Application and IT Component fact sheets that captures AI usage type, risk, approval state, and AI potential; a new LLM subtype on IT Components for first-class modeling of large language models; an AI Potential Assessment on Business Capability fact sheets; and a Technology Assessment and AI Radar Classification for standardized categorization. Attributes support EU AI Act risk-tier classification (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal).

The MCP Server (generally available in 2025) is described in LeanIX marketing as: "To allow non-technical users to access EA data with natural-language prompts… we launched the SAP LeanIX MCP server in 2025… This is AI-native enterprise architecture and it's now available in SAP LeanIX." The Joule integration exposes an embedded diamond-icon Joule panel inside SAP LeanIX for conversational search across fact sheets, diagrams, reports, and documentation, marketed as "Joule with SAP LeanIX" and included with a Joule Base subscription. The Joule Studio Agent builder had planned general availability in December 2025 with hooks into LeanIX metadata for secure agent permissions and CI/CD pipeline telemetry.

Customer quotes on LeanIX AI are almost uniformly positive. A G2 review from 21 November 2025 says: "I love how SAP LeanIX gives full visibility into your IT landscape — it's collaborative, data-driven, and AI-enabled, helping you drive architecture decisions and streamline transformation efficiently." A mid-size manufacturer with 5,000 employees says of the AI Assistant: "This is a differentiating feature, enabling Architects and their stakeholders to find the information they're looking for in a much more natural and faster way." A manufacturing customer with 40,000 employees says: "Really great that LeanIX enables this feature and shares it at an early stage; first version looks already very promising." LeanIX product copy summarizes the positioning: "Leverage AI throughout SAP LeanIX and move from documentation to decision-making. Spend less time searching, more time deciding."

### 5.2 Ardoq AI capabilities

Ardoq's AI story is built on the "New Ardoq Experience" that unifies Discover with the Core platform, with the new experience enabled by default for customers created after 8 April 2026. ### 5.5 Hallucination context

Accuracy is a shared risk across all AI-powered EA tools. GPT-4 and GPT-4o hallucination rates range from approximately 1.5 percent on the HalluLens benchmark to 20 to 50 percent or higher in adversarial or open-ended tasks; TruthfulQA reported GPT-4 at 57 percent truthful accuracy; a reference-generation study found GPT-4 hallucinated 28.6 percent of references versus GPT-3.5's 39.6 percent. When grounded via RAG on curated data such as a Cancer Information Service study, hallucination rates dropped to 0 to 6 percent. Enterprise tools such as Ardoq and LeanIX that use RAG over their own validated repositories are expected to fall at the low end of that spectrum, but workshop attendees should be aware that the underlying model is not perfect and that governance mechanisms (approval workflows, human-in-the-loop review) remain essential.

## 7. Win/Loss Patterns and Competitive Positioning

### 7.1 The head-to-head pattern

When LeanIX and Ardoq compete head-to-head, the pattern is consistent enough to be predictive. LeanIX wins when the buyer prioritizes fast time-to-value, simple onboarding, alignment with the SAP ecosystem (RISE with SAP, S/4HANA, Signavio, WalkMe), APM-first use cases (rationalization, cloud migration, technology risk, cost optimization), unlimited-user pricing that structurally enables broad adoption, and Gartner-standard rationalization frameworks (TIME and 6R). Ardoq wins when the buyer prioritizes flexibility, custom metamodel, coverage across all four EA domains (Business, Application, Technology, Data), graph-based dependency analysis, scenario-based target-state modeling, and a distinctive crowdsourcing loop (Broadcasts plus Discover plus Surveys plus Approvals) — particularly in high-maturity EA teams or organizations that have previously used Sparx Enterprise Architect and want a modern SaaS replacement without giving up modeling depth.

### 7.2 TCO and pricing signals

Independent 3-year TCO estimates cluster around USD 120,000 for LeanIX and USD 130,000 for Ardoq at mid-market scale, with wide variance based on modules purchased and customer size. LeanIX pricing is per application with unlimited users and tiered by module (APM base, plus TRM, BTM, Cloud Native Suite, etc.), and independent ranges suggest USD 50,000 to USD 200,000+ per year. Ardoq is also priced per application with unlimited users, with features grouped into "outcome-based modules" (portfolio, transformation, planning) plus add-ons such as Sandbox, AI Process Modeling, and Production Instances sold separately, and independent range estimates of approximately USD 40,000 to USD 80,000+ per year starting. Post-SAP-acquisition, LeanIX pricing has been folded into SAP's standard list price framework, meaning legacy discounting patterns no longer apply, and this pricing shift is a specific weapon competitors deploy in deals.

### 7.3 The SAP-acquisition-effect on churn risk

The SAP acquisition of LeanIX has created a documented pattern of neutrality-driven concern that competitors are actively exploiting. E3 Magazine and Reddit commentary raise recurring themes: buyers who chose LeanIX for its neutrality now question whether SAP will over-index the product toward SAP-only use cases, whether integration with non-SAP CMDBs, ITSMs, and cloud platforms will atrophy, whether the product roadmap will be captured by the RISE with SAP go-to-market rather than serving broader EA needs, and whether pricing under SAP will remain competitive versus quote-based Ardoq deals. So far the evidence points the other way — LeanIX has continued shipping non-SAP integrations, and the AI Agent Hub explicitly supports Microsoft and Google agents — but perception matters, and the workshop should assume Ardoq salespeople will keep raising it.

### 7.4 Ardoq's go-to-market momentum

Ardoq has more than 400 customers, more than 140 partners, and a striking 50 percent partner-led new business ratio. The Series D round of USD 125 million in March 2022 was led by EQT Growth with One Peak participating, at a post-money valuation "over 300 million dollars" per TechCrunch. Tracxn lists 2022 revenue at NOK 108 million or approximately USD 11.3 million, implying LeanIX was roughly ten times Ardoq's revenue at the time of the SAP deal — a substantial scale gap that Ardoq is closing on the back of approximately 80 percent year-on-year ARR growth reported for 2021 and a new Chief Revenue Officer hired to accelerate US expansion. Ardoq's September 2024 acquisition of ShiftX for process visualization complements the graph-native scenario model, and the recent GraphLake acquisition creates a neuro-symbolic RDF plus LPG stack that positions Ardoq as technically distinctive.

## 8. Emerging Threats and Disruptors

### 8.1 AI-native EA startups

There is not yet a single dominant AI-native EA disruptor, but the seed and early-stage landscape shows a set of distinctive philosophical bets. Catio, founded in 2023 with approximately USD 7 million raised from RRE and Eniac, markets itself as an "AI Architecture IDE" anchored on an Archie agent that generates and reviews architecture with continuous feedback loops from code and cloud state. Boldo, a French graph-native EA startup, targets the flexible-metamodel philosophical territory Ardoq already occupies but with a more code-native developer experience. Ardoq itself increasingly reads as an AI-native pivot rather than a pure-play EA vendor, and its GraphLake acquisition — creating a neuro-symbolic architecture that combines RDF triples and labeled property graphs — is being positioned as a differentiated reasoning substrate for AI queries against the EA repository.

More broadly, adjacent AI-native enterprise infrastructure has seen substantial funding in 2024 and 2025: Convey raised USD 38 million Series A (a16z) for enterprise AI workflow automation; Orkes raised USD 40 million Series B for enterprise orchestration of AI workloads; Vellum raised USD 25 million Series A for production AI orchestration; Ethos raised USD 22.75 million Series A (a16z) for AI agent infrastructure; UnifyApps raised USD 20 million (Iconiq Growth, November 2024) plus another USD 20 to 25 million in negotiation with WestBridge for AI-led SaaS integration; and Emergent AI raised approximately USD 20 million (Lightspeed) for an AI-backed enterprise app development platform. Sapphire Ventures reports enterprise software captured 52 percent of all VC funding in 2025 (up from 41 percent in 2024 and approximately 29 percent over the prior decade), with GenAI-native applications receiving USD 8.5 billion through October 2024. None of these companies is an EA-native play, but they represent the pool from which future disruptors will emerge.

### 8.2 Developer portals encroaching on EA

The larger structural threat is not a new EA startup but the developer portal category, which increasingly owns the software catalog wedge that once anchored EA. Backstage (the Spotify open-source portal), Cortex, OpsLevel, Roadie (managed Backstage), Atlassian Compass, and Port.io all provide a service and application catalog with ownership, scorecards, and templates that overlap meaningfully with the APM subset of EA. Atlassian's decision to deprecate Compass Templates in December 2025 illustrates the market's messiness but does not blunt the broader trend. For SAP LeanIX, the risk is that engineering-led organizations adopt a developer portal for their software catalog, treating LeanIX as a stakeholder-visibility layer rather than a system of record, at which point the LeanIX license becomes discretionary. The defensive move is deep, first-class Backstage and Compass integration positioned as complementary rather than competitive.

### 8.3 ServiceNow and CMDB encroachment

ServiceNow's 2024 pivot to a full-fledged EA module — leveraging its ITSM, CMDB, and CSDM installed base — is a meaningful platform-play. Some LeanIX customers already run ServiceNow APM alongside LeanIX; ServiceNow's Alignment Planner, Strategic Portfolio Management, and AI Control Tower create the possibility of a single-vendor consolidation story. The LeanIX countermove is complementary positioning: LeanIX as the EA repository and decision-management layer, ServiceNow as the ITSM and CMDB source of truth, with bidirectional integration.

### 8.4 Analyst commentary on market disruption

Gartner reports a 9.5 percent year-on-year increase in EA tool interest, and Forrester's Q4 2025 commentary characterizes the market as consolidating rather than fragmenting, with the winning formula described as "AI plus integration plus governance in one platform." That framing is favorable to LeanIX given the AI Agent Hub, MCP Server, SAP Business AI integration, and the AI Governance metamodel extension — but Ardoq is credibly executing the same recipe with the AI Gateway, Omnipresent AI Assistant, and its GraphLake foundation. The Gartner note that only ten vendors were assessed in the 2025 Magic Quadrant, with three new entrants and five departures, underscores that the market is narrowing to a small handful of survivors.

## 9. Strategic Implications for the LeanIX Team

### 9.1 Defend the AI Governance moat

The AI Agent Hub and MCP Server together give SAP LeanIX a genuine first-mover position on AI governance, and the pricing decision to include them in existing subscriptions is a strong go-to-market weapon. The workshop should treat this as a defend-and-extend priority: continue deepening the AI Governance metamodel extension (LLM subtype on IT Components, AI Potential Assessment on Business Capability, EU AI Act risk tiers), invest heavily in the AI Agent Radar as an executive-communication artifact, and lock in the perception that "SAP LeanIX is the AI governance cockpit for the enterprise" before competitors reach parity. The competitive risk is that pure-play AI governance vendors (Credo AI, OneTrust, IBM watsonx.governance, Holistic AI) offer deeper model observability and bias testing than LeanIX plans; LeanIX's counter is portfolio integration and a strategic partnership pattern with one or two of those vendors for the deep controls.

### 9.2 Modernize the Architecture Decisions feature aggressively

The 2025 launch of Architecture Decisions in LeanIX is the right feature at the right time, but it is competing against Ardoq's roughly four-year head start and against Structurizr's Git-native ADR pattern. The workshop should prioritize three things: shipping the AI-based architecture guidance overlay that the Q2 2025 roadmap promised, deepening the diagram embedding for scenario comparison that late 2025 announcements previewed, and building a Git-native round-trip so that Markdown ADRs in a repository can synchronize with LeanIX Architecture Decisions. That third bet would leapfrog Ardoq's URL-based Confluence integration and win the developer-heavy accounts that currently see EA tools as bureaucracy.

### 9.3 Close the Business Architecture and reporting-polish gaps

Reviewer criticism of LeanIX clusters consistently around three issues: metamodel rigidity (particularly for Business Architecture), reporting polish (dashboards "not visually appealing enough to present to C-level stakeholders"), and diagram-editor immaturity ("copy-paste only recently became available in beta"). The workshop should treat these as sales-blocking table stakes rather than nice-to-haves. Reviewer verdicts such as "LeanIX sucks at Business Architecture, but is great at Technology, Applications and Integration" are competitive kryptonite in accounts where the buyer is a Business Architect rather than an APM lead. The rebuttal to competitors' talk track "LeanIX will lock you into a rigid metamodel" needs to be a demonstrable controlled extensibility story, not just a slide claim.

### 9.4 Neutralize the SAP-acquisition perception

Ardoq salespeople are actively exploiting the neutrality narrative in deals: "SAP will make LeanIX SAP-only." The evidence points the other way — LeanIX has continued shipping non-SAP integrations, and the AI Agent Hub explicitly supports Microsoft and Google agents. The workshop should agree on a crisp set of proof points: named non-SAP customer wins post-acquisition, a public commitment to non-SAP integration parity, and case studies with ServiceNow, Atlassian, and hyperscaler-native accounts. Pricing perception is a related issue where transparency about the SAP list-price framework combined with credible discount patterns will materially help field sales.

### 9.5 Own the "AI plus integration plus governance in one platform" narrative

Forrester's framing of the winning formula as "AI plus integration plus governance in one platform" is the exact positioning LeanIX should adopt. AI is the AI Agent Hub, MCP Server, and Enterprise Architecture Assistant. Integration is the SAP ecosystem plus documented Backstage, Cortex, Port.io, ServiceNow, Atlassian, and hyperscaler compatibility. Governance is Architecture Decisions plus the AI Governance and Adoption metamodel extension plus EU AI Act risk tiering plus the AI Agent Radar. The workshop's job is to translate these three pillars into a single narrative that a CIO can repeat to a board, a director can defend to a procurement committee, and a sales rep can deliver in a first meeting.

### 9.6 Watch the developer portal category

The developer portal encroachment is the least-discussed but potentially most disruptive long-run threat, because it changes who controls the software catalog. The strategic play is complementary integration positioning: LeanIX as the enterprise system of record, Backstage or Compass as the engineer's daily-driver catalog, with bidirectional sync so that a developer marking a service deprecated in Backstage automatically updates the LeanIX Application fact sheet. If that integration story is credible and shipped, the developer portal category becomes an ally that expands LeanIX's data quality rather than a competitor that shrinks its footprint.
